# Match-Type Taxonomy

The archetypes the `match-type-classifier` assigns. The type determines which logic the
`match-analyst` applies. Getting this right is the highest-leverage step in the pipeline —
applying blowout logic to a coin flip (or vice versa) is the root cause of bad calls.

Each type lists: how to recognise it, the logic that fires, and the clean-sheet / margin
stance.

---

## Type A — Clear mismatch, SOFT underdog

**Recognise:** Large ranking/probability gap (favourite often 75%+). Underdog is in
freefall — leaky defence, morale collapse, recently sacked coach, shipping goals in bunches.
No stable scoring ability.

**Logic:** Extreme signals → extreme conclusion (Rule 2). The underdog gets pinned back
and starved; its attackers, however named, rarely see the ball.

**Stance:** **Clean sheet = LOCK. Push for a big margin** (3–0, 4–0, 5–0 territory).
**Do NOT gift a consolation goal.**

*Example pattern:* an already-eliminated side with the worst defence in the field, playing
a fired-up favourite. Predicted underdog goals here have repeatedly failed to appear.

---

## Type B — Clear mismatch, HARD underdog

**Recognise:** Clear favourite, but the underdog is a disciplined, compact low block that
defends well and has kept recent games close, even in defeat. Physical, organised, hard to
break down. Lacks its own firepower.

**Logic:** Favourite's difficulty breaking a low block matters (watch for a history of high
possession + low shots on target). The underdog won't be humiliated but also won't create
much.

**Stance:** **Favourite wins, but NARROW.** Clean sheet plausible but not required.
**Do NOT inflate the margin** — this is where over-big predictions die.

*Example pattern:* a favourite that just got frustrated 0–0 by a similar low block, now
facing another one. The honest call is a 1–0 / 2–0 grind, not a rout.

---

## Type C — Strong favourite, LEAKY-BUT-DANGEROUS underdog

**Recognise:** Favourite clearly better, but the underdog can *genuinely create and convert*
— real chances in recent games (shots on target, big chances), a live transition or
set-piece threat, and a defence loose enough that the favourite also concedes. The key: the
underdog's threat is backed by **evidence of ball control / chance creation**, not just
famous names.

**Logic:** Both sides likely score. Favourite's superior quality still tells, but a clean
sheet is unlikely.

**Stance:** **Favourite wins; both teams score; medium-to-big margin** (2–1, 3–1 territory).
Here — and only here — an underdog goal is *earned and expected*, not a gift. State the
evidence explicitly so the bias auditor passes it.

*Example pattern:* a top side with a slightly leaky/injured defence vs. an underdog with two
genuine goal threats who actually get service. Also fits a favourite vs. a side that has
never kept a World Cup clean sheet and scores from set pieces via a height advantage.

---

## Type D — Two strong sides, COIN FLIP

**Recognise:** ~50/50. Both strong, both in form, small or no probability gap (e.g. 45–48%
each with a fat draw slice). Often a marquee knockout.

**Logic:** Randomness dominates the *result* even when you read the *game* well. In knockouts,
120 minutes and penalties are on the table, and a shootout is close to a coin flip.

**Stance:** **Name the randomness.** Give a lean if you have one, but flag real
extra-time/penalty risk and DO NOT force a confident winner. Weigh hidden habits: a side that
leads then concedes; a team with genuine big-match penalty pedigree. Goal expectation depends
on both defences — could be open or cagey; judge on the specific sides.

*Example pattern:* two top-ten sides in the Round of 16. The accurate prediction is "tight,
plausibly extra time, either could go through," not a made-up scoreline.

---

## Type E — Two strong sides, one only needs a DRAW

**Recognise:** Two reasonably matched sides, but asymmetric stakes — one side advances/tops
the group with a draw and will play pragmatically; the other must chase.

**Logic:** The side needing only a draw sits deeper and kills the game; the chaser may lack
the tools to break a set defence. This suppresses goals.

**Stance:** **Elevated low-scoring and draw probability.** A 0–0 or 1–0 / 1–1 grind is very
much in play. Do not assume the "better" side runs away with it — the pragmatic side often
gets exactly the result it needs.

*Example pattern:* two strong teams where a draw sends one through as group winner; frequently
ends cagey, sometimes goalless.

---

## Classification guidance

- Assign **one primary** type; note a **secondary** only if genuinely borderline.
- The hardest and most important discrimination is **A vs. B vs. C** — i.e. *which kind of
  weak* the underdog is. Always resolve it with the **ball-control / chance-creation test**,
  not with attacker reputation.
- **D and E** are about *two strong sides*; the differentiator is whether stakes are
  symmetric (D) or one side only needs a draw (E).
- If data is thin, classify on what's available and flag the uncertainty — do not stall.

## Quick reference

| Type | Underdog is… | Clean sheet | Margin | Both score? |
|------|--------------|-------------|--------|-------------|
| A | soft / collapsing | LOCK | big | no |
| B | hard low block | plausible | narrow | usually no |
| C | leaky but dangerous | unlikely | med–big | yes (earned) |
| D | (two strong, 50/50) | either | tight | depends |
| E | (two strong, one needs draw) | likely-ish | tight/low | often no |
