- **Game shape:** Tight through regulation, with a live path to a **wider Colombia margin late** once Ghana is forced to open up and the heat tells. Modal 1-0, secondary 2-0 — deliberately **not** inflated to 3-0/4-0 (a Queiroz block will grind).

Applying the must-win lesson (BIAS-10), the clean sheet is a **lean, not a lock** — a Ghana goal is elevated-and-live once the block opens — but when it does open, a **Colombia second goal is more likely than a Ghana first**, so BTTS-no stays the call. Not gifted, not dismissed.
- **Extra-time / penalty risk (knockout):** Low-medium (~13% Ghana advance), via a 0-0/1-0 stalemate held into extra time. But the **weather cuts against Ghana holding that long**: a 95-100°F heat index and a very high lightning-delay risk both erode a low block's discipline (a cold restart breaks compact shape more than it disrupts a technical favourite). A shootout would be near-random.

## Why (2–4 sentences)

Colombia are a decisive favourite who conceded once all group stage, generated 24 shots against Portugal, and carry a live, in-form attack (Díaz, James, Muñoz from right-back); direction is high-confidence. Ghana are a genuine hard block — Queiroz-drilled, two clean sheets, keeper Asare excellent — but the block is the *depleted* version and, crucially, **Kudus's absence leaves them with almost no way to create in open play** (0.79 xG/game). The must-win format is the one thing that animates their attack: once behind they have to abandon the block, which is exactly when a set-piece or Semenyo counter becomes live — so the clean sheet is a lean, not a lock. But their ceiling is so low that the forced-open phase is more likely to yield a *second Colombia* goal than a first Ghana one, and the Kansas City heat and storm-delay risk both punish the shallower, shape-dependent side.
